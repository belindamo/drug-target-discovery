Chapter 1

A disease with few options and fewer answers

Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis replaces healthy lung with scar tissue. Median survival is 3–5 years from diagnosis. Three drugs are approved — pirfenidone, nintedanib, and nerandomilast — but none halt disease progression, and none were designed around genetic evidence.

Meanwhile, the IPF clinical pipeline is busy. Dozens of trials are running. But a recurring pattern has emerged: high-profile drugs keep failing. Bexotegrast was stopped by its safety board. Galectin-3 inhibitor GB0139 performed worse than placebo. Roche wrote off $400M on zinpentraxin. All three lacked genetic support.

We asked a simple question: what do the genetics actually say?

Chapter 2

A genetics-first pipeline, built entirely from public data

We constructed a systematic pipeline that starts from genome-wide association studies and narrows to actionable drug targets. Every step uses published, public data — no lab experiments, no proprietary datasets. The pipeline applies progressively stricter causal filters: statistical association, then expression mediation, then colocalization, then causal inference, then druggability, then safety.

1
GWAS Locus Curation
33 genome-wide significant loci from 7 studies, GRCh38 coordinates verified against dbSNP. Largest: Chin 2025 (5,159 cases). Strongest signal: MUC5B rs35705950 (OR = 4.11, p < 10-121).
2
Transcriptome-Wide Association
25 gene-tissue associations via S-PrediXcan and OTTERS framework against GTEx v8 lung, blood, and fibroblast models.
3
Bayesian Colocalization
25 colocalization tests (coloc, eCAVIAR, SMR). Threshold: PP.H4 > 0.8 or CLPP > 0.5. Strongest: MUC5B (1.00), ATP11A in smoker lung (0.996), DPP9 in fibroblasts (0.968).
4
Mendelian Randomization
21 causal gene-disease links tested with IVW, MR-Egger, and HEIDI pleiotropy filtering. Key: IL-7 protective (OR = 0.67), BTRC strongly protective (OR = 0.30).
5
Druggability Assessment
Open Targets tractability (up to 10 buckets across SM/AB/PR/OC), ChEMBL compound review, clinical-stage drug identification. Most tractable: ACVRL1 (10 buckets).
6
Safety Screening
DepMap 25Q3 CRISPR Chronos scores for all targets. None are common essential. Safety flags: KIF15 (8.4% dependent lines), SPDL1 (inferred essential).
7
Clinical Gap Analysis
Cross-reference against all active IPF trials on ClinicalTrials.gov (March 2026). Result: ~83% of pipeline programs lack genetic support.
Chapter 3

The pipeline's single most striking finding

DPP9 ranks second in combined genetic evidence. It has a GWAS signal at p = 1.7 × 10-12 and colocalizes beautifully with fibroblast eQTLs (PP.H4 = 0.968). Cryo-EM structures show exactly how DPP9 keeps the NLRP1 inflammasome in check — it sequesters freed NLRP1 fragments like a bomb defuser.

But here's the paradox: you cannot inhibit DPP9 to treat IPF. Inhibiting DPP9 activates NLRP1, triggers pyroptosis, floods the tissue with IL-1β, and worsens fibrosis. Homozygous Dpp9 mutant mice die within 24 hours.

The breakthrough comes from the rescue experiments. Deleting just one copy of the IL-1 receptor (Il-1r) is enough to save those mice. This means the fibrotic damage flows through a single bottleneck: IL-1β.

Three FDA-approved IL-1β inhibitors exist — anakinra, canakinumab, and rilonacept — with over 10,000 patient-years of safety data across rheumatology and autoinflammatory disease. None have ever been tested in IPF.

This is the pipeline's most actionable finding: a genetically anchored drug target (DPP9 → NLRP1 → IL-1β), a clear mechanism, and approved drugs sitting on the shelf.

"The most impactful opportunities are not novel molecules — they are approved drugs that have never been tested in the right disease."
Chapter 4

Five opportunities the field is missing

Beyond IL-1β, the pipeline identified four additional targets where genetically supported interventions are either already approved or in advanced clinical development — yet have never been tested in IPF.

#1 — Strongest signal

MUC5B promoter variant → SPL5B inhaled ASO

The rs35705950 variant (OR = 4.11) is the single strongest genetic risk factor for any common fibrotic disease. It drives MUC5B overexpression in distal airways. SpliSense's SPL5B antisense oligonucleotide, entering Phase 1 in 2026, is the first therapy designed to directly target this signal. Platform validated by SPL84 success in cystic fibrosis.

Coloc PP.H4 = 1.00 Phase 1 entering 2026 Tier 1 evidence
#2 — Biggest translational gap

DPP9 → NLRP1 → IL-1β blockade

DPP9 loss de-represses the NLRP1 inflammasome, driving IL-1β-mediated fibrosis. Anakinra or canakinumab could test this pathway tomorrow. The Harapas 2022 rescue experiment (deleting one copy of IL-1R saves DPP9-null mice) is the key translational evidence. Zero IPF trials exist for any IL-1 inhibitor.

Coloc PP.H4 = 0.968 Zero IPF trials FDA-approved drugs available
#3 — Best repurposing candidate

IL-7 → Recombinant CYT107

Mendelian randomization shows IL-7 is protective (OR = 0.67). Mechanistically, IL-7 inhibits fibroblast TGF-β signaling via JAK1/STAT1/Smad7. CYT107 has been safely dosed in over 650 patients, including COVID lung injury. DepMap shows 0% dependency — an exceptionally clean safety profile.

MR OR = 0.67 protective Zero IPF trials 0% DepMap dependency
#4 — Genetically correct mTOR strategy

DEPTOR + NPRL3 → Sirolimus

Two independent GWAS loci (DEPTOR and NPRL3) converge on mTORC1 hyperactivation. Everolimus failed in IPF — but everolimus inhibits both mTORC1 and mTORC2. Sirolimus is mTORC1-selective, which is the genetically indicated mechanism. A pilot study already showed 35% fibrocyte suppression.

Two GWAS loci converge Pilot data exists FDA-approved
#5 — Biomarker becomes target

MUC1-CT → GO-203 peptide inhibitor

MUC1 (KL-6) is widely used as an IPF biomarker. But it's also a functional driver: MUC1-CT phosphorylation activates fibrotic gene programs via a TGF-β1/Smad3/β-catenin complex. KO mice are protected. Pirfenidone partially works through MUC1-CT. The Chin 2025 GWAS locus (OR = 1.162) now provides genetic evidence.

KO mice protected GO-203 Phase I/II (AML) Theranostic: KL-6 + GO-203
Chapter 5

The pattern in recent failures

Three of the five most prominent IPF clinical programs to report results in 2024–2025 failed. All three targeted genes without genome-wide significant GWAS support. The two that succeeded — nerandomilast (PDE4B, approved) and treprostinil (prostacyclin, +95.6 mL FVC) — also lacked direct genetic support, so this is not a universal rule. But the pattern is consistent with the 2–3× success enrichment for genetically supported targets observed across therapeutic areas.

Chapter 8

Beyond European genetics

Over 90% of IPF GWAS data comes from European-ancestry populations. The All of Us analysis (2025) offers an early look at portability: non-telomere IPF pathways transfer better across ancestries (PRS OR 1.35 in non-EUR vs 1.29 in EUR), while telomere-length signals are diminished. The MUC5B promoter variant has similar effect size across groups (~OR 3.2) but dramatically lower allele frequency in non-European populations (50-fold lower in African ancestry).

Implication: Drug development targeting inflammasome, mTOR, and immune pathways may benefit more diverse global populations than telomere-focused approaches. TERT and DSP appear most broadly relevant across ancestries.

Chapter 9

Three trials that need no new drugs

The most striking feature of these findings is their immediacy. No novel drug development is required. Each proposed trial uses an approved agent with extensive safety data.

Trial 1: Anakinra + standard of care in IPF

Phase 2a, 12 weeks. Enrich for high baseline BAL IL-1β. Stratify by DPP9 genotype (rs12610495). Primary endpoint: FVC change. Biomarkers: serum IL-1β, IL-18, KL-6. Rationale: DPP9 GWAS + NLRP1 structural biology + Il-1r haploinsufficiency rescue.

Trial 2: CYT107 + standard of care in IPF

Phase 2a, 16 weeks. PK/PD in IPF lung. Assess TGF-β pathway biomarkers and fibrocyte counts. Safety already demonstrated in >650 patients including COVID lung. Rationale: MR OR = 0.67 + JAK1/STAT1/Smad7 mechanism.

Trial 3: Sirolimus + standard of care in IPF

Phase 2b, 52 weeks. Powered for FVC. Biomarker: fibrocyte count, mTOR activity in PBMCs. Key design point: distinguish from failed everolimus (mTORC1+2) by demonstrating mTORC1-selective biology. Rationale: two independent GWAS loci (DEPTOR + NPRL3) + pilot 35% fibrocyte suppression.
